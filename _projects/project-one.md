I directed a production of Rhinoceros at the Kings Players, the theatre society within King's College London. Rhinoceros is about a small French town where, one by one, the inhabitants start turning into rhinos. Underneath its absurdity, it's a play about fascism and conformity, and the process by which ordinary people come to justify the unjustifiable.

The special effects of this play meant getting extremely creative: how do you turn an actor into a rhinoceros live on stage? Turns out, with clever lighting, a lot of green paint and cardboard. I really enjoyed leaning into the deliberately lo-fi feel, as I felt it was well-matched to the themes of the play: a world with visible seams matched its logic far better than realism ever could. It was also the wisest choice financially. With our budget, "realistic" would have meant a few expensive pieces of stage furniture and nothing else. Committing fully to the homemade aesthetic meant every corner of the stage belonged to the same world. Our set looked like a French cartoon!

The play didn't come without some hiccoups, namely our lead actor unforutnately having to leave the production about 3 weeks before opening night. That meant some quick thinking; rearranging the cast, a new rehearsal schedule and lots of discussions with the actors to get them familiar with their new roles! Fortunately Rhinoceros is play with 14 characters, designed to be play by just 8; during my casting, I had the good foresight to cast 9, just in case. In hindsight, that spare actor may have been one of the most important decisions I made. 

This production taught me more about leading a team than anything else I've ever done. Also how to remove green paint from clothing. In the end, Rhinoceros was a huge success, and I'd do it all again in a heartbeat!
