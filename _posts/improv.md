---
title: What running improv taught me
teaser: Fish make excellent metaphors
---

Growing up, I was always involved in amateur theatre one way or another. I've played every variation of Zip Zap Zop, Zip Zap Boing, Boing Pow Bing, whatever you want to call it. So when I became the head of improv sessions for the King's Players, I felt pretty prepared. I know how to run games and be silly!

Looking back now, I can say that running those sessions taught me a lot more than that Wink Murder is, ultimately, a pretty boring game. I believe improv is an untapped source of creative freedom and self-exploration that most people either aren't aware of or are unwilling to try. With that in mind, I've decided to explain everything I've learned from leading an improv class, in the hopes that you'll either try improv, or maybe absorb everything I've gained through osmosis. I hope you do the first one, though.

## The power of accepting your thoughts

Perhaps the Cartesian Theatre model of the mind isn't entirely philosophically sound, but as a way of interpreting and accepting your own
thoughts, I find it extremely useful. My mind is a thing I only have partial view and control over; the backend is a hobbledegobble of components working together to give me the sensation of a coherent perspective of reality. I believe the first thing improv teaches you (besides the age-old "yes and," which you'll notice I've deigned not to make a low-hanging pun about in this post) is to accept and embrace the back-end of your mind.

What this means in practice is allowing ideas to exist without claiming ownership or judgement over them. Whenever an idea pops into my head, I accept that it arrived from many complex causes: the media I've consumed, the experiences I've had, maybe even what I ate that day. I don't take ownership of these ideas. I'm just a witness to them.

This is best captured by a wonderful metaphor from David Lynch, who argues that you don't "invent" ideas but catch them, like fish. Your mind is a pond; small ideas swim near the surface, but there are more powerful concepts in the deep end.

The way I take this is: as the conscious part of your mind, it isn't your job to make ideas exist. They're all in there already. Rather, you get the refining task: catching them, combining them, tossing some back into the pond.

Improv is powerful because it teaches you to see fish better. A little one swimming near the surface, perhaps slightly obscured by the murkiness of the water. In the moment of a scene, improv tells you to grab that one, pull it out, and start descaling it right then and there. It leaves no time to get picky about ideas, and in doing so removes any judgement over your own mind and the fish it hands you. Instead, it lets the conscious you get better at all the front-end fish tasks.

## Reading a room

For the first few weeks of improv, I came in with a notebook with the activities we'd be doing jotted down. I'd plan down to the minute: 10
minutes warming up, 25 minutes of game one, 20 of game two. On the one hand, I was extremely prepared. Isn't that a good thing?

In fact, this strict routine was actually inhibiting the natural rhythms that came from what I talked about in point one. In a room of people
harvesting fish, sometimes this would extend to the games themselves, and we'd invent new twists on the activities we were doing. Sometimes a scene would transform beyond its original prompt, and it would be brilliant. I'd been letting improv be freeform within a strict structure, and the walls of that structure had more impact than I'd realised.

In light of this, I developed a more reactive approach to our sessions. I'd come in with a loose idea of the arc I wanted the session to take (broadly starting with large group games, for example, to create comfort and an inclusive feel, then honing down into small-group scenes to build specific scene skills), and I'd always bring my (long) list of improv games with me. But the actual games we played, and how long they lasted, would depend far more on watching the group and reading their responses, anything from the general energy of the room to how many newcomers were there that day.

Learning to see a lack of structure as freeing, rather than as a bad thing, did wonders for our improv group. It also brought out a collaborative feel, with people offering up ideas for their own favourite activities, and even new ones to try.

## Inclusivity as an invisible force

I knew from day one that I wanted my improv sessions to be open to all. There should be no barriers to entry, mental or physical. I made it my
mission to constantly, consciously work toward this, while making those decisions feel unconscious to everyone else: a given, not something that ever needs to be asked for. This started simply: always having chairs available in the room for sitting on, no questions asked. I'd also keep a chair within the scene space itself, so performers could sit down during a scene.

Inclusivity also means paying attention to potential pressures. One of the most important things improv has taught me, something I'll apply to all areas of my life, is this: in a potentially nerve-wracking situation, people prefer not opting out over opting in. Opting in is active: it means signing up to do something potentially scary, like joining the three-person alphabet scene. Not opting out is passive: "yeah, I don't mind doing the scary thing." There's a distinct difference mentally, one I noticed immediately after introducing my "heart method." Instead of asking for volunteers for a scene, I'd pick participants myself. Combined with my own growing awareness of the group (who was a regular, who took part more), this gave me the freedom to introduce new scene partners to each other, play to people's strengths, and challenge them. The heart method worked like this: if someone didn't want to be picked, they'd put their hands together in a heart shape on their lap, subtle and inconspicuous. I'd take note of this, and it also gave me the chance to later pick those same people for other activities when they weren't making the heart shape.

This took the pressure off more hesitant people, since it's much easier to let yourself be picked than to volunteer for something. It's a strategy I'll definitely carry forward with me.

## My advice 

Join an improv class! If you don't feel comfortable with that idea yet however, here's a game I recommend that you can play with a group of any size. No improv experience required! 

**Wise, Wise, Wise:** 

The group gathers in a circle. One by one, each person says a single word with as little thinking time as possible. Together, they are trying to create something that resembles a philosophical aphorism. When the group collectively decides they've completed one (regardless of whether they've gone around the full circle, or gone round it multiple times), they all nod their heads and pensively say "wise, wise, wise". Whoever ended the last aphorism starts the next one! 

These don't need to be real philosophical truths and observations. In fact, more often than not, they will be ridiculous! I love this game because it embodies exactly what I've been trying to getting at: in improv, nobody is trying to be right. You're just catching whatever swims by, and trusting that it'll turn out wise, wise, wise.
